Food Security Indicators Around The World

What is food insecurity?

Food insecurity is defined by the UN FAO as the “situation when people lack secure access to sufficient amounts of safe and nutritious food for normal growth and development and an active and healthy life.”

The prevalence of undernourishment is the main hunger indicator used by the UN's Food and Agriculture Organization, and is the share of people who do not meet the minimum dietary intake.

Severe food insecurity results in undernourishment and hunger. Causes of severe food insecurity include food being unavailable or unaffordable, and unbalanced distribution of food among households.

The Pacific Islands, such as Nauru, have the highest rates of obesity in the world. This can be attributed to the reliance on imported western food, which are high in calories and sodium, and cultural factors, such as poor public education on health. Obesity is seen as a sign of wealth, and higher social status is given to imported foods rather than local, healthier foods.

The other side of hunger: Obesity

While lack of food causes undernourishment, unhealthy diets can lead to obesity and diet-related chronic diseases, such as cardiovascular disease, diabetes and cancer.

The causes of obesity go beyond poor eating habits, and factors such as overconsumption and limited access to quality food play a part. Nutritional intake in the first 1000 days of a child’s life is also a risk factor.

Too little weight-for-height

Generally a sign of rapid weight loss, a child is considered wasted if their weight-for-height is more than 2 standard deviations below the median for WHO’s Child Growth Standards. The cause is related to poor nutrition, feeding practices and inadequate sanitary conditions.

Too much weight-for-height

A child is overweight if their weight-for-height greater than 2 standard deviations above the median for WHO’s Child Growth Standards. Being overweight or obese is linked to more deaths worldwide than being underweight, however it is largely preventable by choosing healthier foods and exercise.

Too little height-for-weight

Stunting is an indicator of severe malnutrition, and children are considered stunted if they have a height which is below the median height-for-age for WHO’s Child Growth Standards. The impacts of stunting on child development are considered to be largely irreversible beyond childhood.
