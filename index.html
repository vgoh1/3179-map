Food Security Indicators Around The World

Source: United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization (UN FAO)

What is food security?

Food insecurity is defined by the UN FAO as the “situation when people lack secure access to sufficient amounts of safe and nutritious food for normal growth and development and an active and healthy life.” Severe food insecurity results in undernourishment and hunger. Causes of severe food insecurity include food being unavailable or unaffordable, and unbalanced distribution of food among households.

UN FAO uses prevalence of undernourishment to measure hunger around the world. The observed regional inequalities, especially in Sub-Saharan Africa, are a result of political conflict (a leading cause of famine), and is compounded by the climate (which can cause drought and flood conditions).

Wasting is generally a sign (especially in children) of rapid weight loss. A child is classified as wasted if they have a height that falls below the median height-for-age of the WHO's Child Growth Standards. The factors which contribute to this weight loss are associated with measures related to both diet and nutrition, and infection. As a result, wasting is often compounded by conditions of poor nutrition, feeding practices as well as inadequate sanitary conditions.

Stunting is an indicator of severe malnutrition. Unlike wasting and low weight-for-age, the impacts of stunting on child development are considered to be largely irreversible beyond the first 1000 days of a child’s life. It can have severe impacts on both cognitive and physical development throughout an individual’s life.
